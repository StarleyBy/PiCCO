Physiological Basis of Hemodynamics and Oxygen Transport

This section discusses key physiological concepts underlying PiCCO parameters and hemodynamic monitoring.

Frank-Starling Law: Theory and Mechanism

The Frank-Starling Law describes the fundamental relationship between the preload stretch of cardiomyocytes and the force of cardiac contraction.

1. Definition

The law states that the heart's stroke volume (SV) increases in response to an increase in the volume of blood filling the ventricles (end-diastolic volume), all other factors being equal.

2. Underlying Mechanism (Sarcomere Length)

The mechanism works exclusively within the normal physiological range of cardiac muscle:

  • Preload and Fiber Length: Increased venous return leads to a greater end-diastolic volume (EDV). This EDV mechanically stretches ventricular fibers (sarcomeres) to a greater length.
  • Optimal Overlap: Stretching sarcomeres to an optimal length:
    • Increases actin-myosin overlap: Leads to a more favorable geometric relationship between thick (myosin) and thin (actin) filaments for tension generation.
    • Increases Ca2+ sensitivity: Critically, stretching the mechanism increases the sensitivity of contractile proteins (troponin C) to available intracellular calcium (Ca2+). This allows stronger binding and a greater number of cross-bridge cycles.
  • Resulting SV: The stretched muscle contracts more forcefully, leading to a larger stroke volume and, consequently, higher cardiac output (CO = SV √ó HR).

3. Clinical Application (Frank-Starling Curve)

The relationship is graphically represented by the Frank-Starling curve:

  • Ascending limb (preload-dependent): Where the muscle is under-stretched (low EDV), SV increases significantly with each increment in EDV (or preload). Patients in this range are fluid responsive.
  • Plateau (preload-independent): Once EDV reaches an optimal point, the curve flattens. Further increases in EDV do not significantly increase SV and may even decrease it. Patients in this range are not fluid responsive, and additional fluid may lead to congestion (EVLWI ‚Üë).

In PiCCO monitoring:

  • Preload surrogate: Global End-Diastolic Volume Index (GEDVI) is a surrogate for EDV.
  • Fluid responsiveness: Stroke Volume Variation (SVV) / Pulse Pressure Variation (PPV) determine if the heart is on the ascending limb or plateau.

Preload: Theory and Mechanism

Preload is the mechanical state of the heart at the end of diastole, representing the degree of stretch of myocardial fibers before contraction (systole). It is a key determinant of stroke volume (SV) according to the Frank-Starling law.

1. Definition and Physiological Basis

Definition: Preload is the end-diastolic volume (EDV) that stretches the right and left ventricles to their maximal capacity.

Mechanism: Increased preload stretches the ventricular muscle fibers, optimizing the overlap between actin and myosin. This enhances the sensitivity of the contractile apparatus to calcium (Ca2+), leading to a stronger force of contraction and a larger SV.

2. Clinical Measurement and Surrogates

Preload cannot be measured directly in clinical practice; volumetric or pressure surrogates must be used:

Category Standard Surrogates PiCCO Surrogates
Static Volume (Gold Standard) Left ventricular end-diastolic volume (by echocardiography) Global End-Diastolic Volume Index (GEDVI), Intrathoracic Blood Volume Index (ITBVI)
Static Pressure (Less Reliable) CVP (Central Venous Pressure) and Pulmonary Artery Occlusion Pressure (PCWP) GEDVI is preferred over pressure indices because volume is less affected by ventricular compliance and intrathoracic pressure.

3. Preload and Fluid Responsiveness

The goal of preload assessment in critical care is to determine if the patient is fluid responsive (i.e., operating on the ascending limb of the Frank-Starling curve).

  • Fluid Responsive (Preload-Dependent): If increasing preload (fluid administration) leads to a significant increase in SV and CI. PiCCO identifies this state using dynamic parameters like Stroke Volume Variation (SVV).
  • Not Fluid Responsive (Preload-Independent): If increasing preload does not lead to a significant increase in SV and only increases the risk of pulmonary edema (EVLWI).

Afterload: Theory and Mechanism

Afterload is the force or stress developed in the ventricular wall during contraction (ejection) phase of the cardiac cycle. It represents the load the ventricle must overcome to eject blood into the systemic circulation.

1. Definition and Determinants

Definition: Afterload is the resistance or impedance that the heart must work against to eject blood.

Primary Determinants:

  1. Systemic Vascular Resistance (SVR): Total resistance in the peripheral arteries (main component).
  2. Aortic Pressure: Pressure in the aorta at the time of aortic valve opening.
  3. Ventricular Wall Stress (Laplace's Law): The stress required in the ventricular muscle wall to generate enough pressure to overcome aortic pressure.

2. Physiological Mechanism

Afterload is a key factor linking cardiac function to the vascular system.

  • Impact on Stroke Volume (SV): High afterload significantly reduces the duration and velocity of ventricular shortening. This leads to a decreased stroke volume (SV), forcing the heart to work harder (increased oxygen consumption) just to maintain cardiac output (CO).
  • Role of Laplace's Law: Stress (T) in the ventricular wall is proportional to internal pressure (P) and chamber radius (r) (T ‚àù P √ó r). A dilated or failing heart (large r) requires proportionally more stress to achieve the same pressure, exacerbating the load and potentially leading to decompensation.

3. Assessment with PiCCO

In clinical practice, afterload is assessed by calculating systemic vascular resistance:

  • PiCCO Parameter: Systemic Vascular Resistance Index (SVRI).
  • Formula: SVRI = (MAP - CVP) / CI √ó 80
  • Therapeutic Goal:
    • High SVRI (Vasoconstriction): Requires vasodilators (to reduce impedance and improve SV). Seen in cardiogenic shock or high doses of vasopressors.
    • Low SVRI (Vasoplegia): Requires vasopressors (to restore MAP and organ perfusion). This is a hallmark of septic shock.

Contractility (Inotropy): Key Theoretical Aspects

Contractility (or inotropy) is the intrinsic ability of the myocardium to develop force and shorten, independent of changes in preload (stretch) and afterload (resistance). It represents the inherent strength of the pump.

1. Definition and Independence

Definition: Contractility is the change in the rate and extent of muscle fiber shortening at any given fiber stretch (preload).

  • Key Distinction: Unlike preload (physical stretch) and afterload (resistance), contractility is an intrinsic property of the muscle fiber itself. Increased contractility shifts the entire Frank-Starling curve up and to the left, meaning the heart can achieve greater SV at any given EDV.

2. Molecular Mechanism (Calcium Cascade)

The most important determinant of myocardial contractility is the availability and handling of intracellular calcium (Ca2+).

  • Sympathetic Activation: The primary extrinsic regulator is the sympathetic nervous system (via norepinephrine/epinephrine), acting on Œ≤-1 adrenergic receptors.
  • Calcium Cascade:
    1. Receptor Activation: Œ≤-1 stimulation increases cyclic AMP (cAMP) concentration.
    2. Ca2+ Entry: cAMP activates protein kinases, which open L-type Ca2+ channels in the sarcolemma. This increases extracellular Ca2+ influx.
    3. Ca2+ Release: A small Ca2+ influx triggers the release of massive amounts of Ca2+ from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) via ryanodine receptors (RyR) (calcium-induced calcium release).
    4. Force Generation: This abundant Ca2+ binds to troponin C, initiating actin-myosin binding, leading to a stronger and faster contraction (increased contractility).

3. Assessment with PiCCO (Load-Independent)

Since contractility cannot be measured directly, it is assessed using derived parameters that attempt to compensate for preload and afterload influences:

  • PiCCO Parameters: Cardiac Function Index (CFI) and Global Ejection Fraction (GEF).
  • Rationale: CFI reflects the volume of blood ejected from the heart per minute relative to the available volume. Low CFI (<4.5) often indicates myocardial failure (e.g., cardiogenic shock or septic cardiomyopathy), requiring inotropic support (drugs that increase Ca2+ availability).

Oxygen Delivery (DO2) and Oxygen Consumption (VO2)

The relationship between DO2 (supply) and VO2 (demand) is fundamental to tissue oxygenation and forms the basis of resuscitation physiology.

1. Theoretical Basis

Oxygen Delivery (DO2) represents the total amount of oxygen transported to systemic tissues per minute. It depends solely on the function of the cardiovascular and pulmonary systems.

Oxygen Consumption (VO2) represents the total amount of oxygen used by tissues per minute. It reflects metabolic needs and the body's energy expenditure.

Oxygen Extraction Ratio (O2ER)

Tissues try to match demand (VO2) to supply (DO2). The efficiency of this match is measured by the Oxygen Extraction Ratio (O2ER):

O2ER = VO2 / DO2

  • Normal O2ER is typically 22% to 30%. Tissues use only a portion of the delivered oxygen, maintaining an oxygen reserve.

2. DO2/VO2 Relationship (Supply Dependence)

The relationship between DO2 and VO2 exists in two critical phases, illustrated by the DO2/VO2 curve:

  1. Supply-independent zone (normal state): DO2 is high, and VO2 remains constant, independent of moderate changes in DO2. Tissues extract only what they need (low O2ER). This is a normal, homeostatic state.
  2. Supply-dependent zone (critical state): When DO2 falls below a critical threshold (critical DO2), tissues have exhausted their extraction reserve, and any further decrease in DO2 directly causes a corresponding decrease in VO2. This state of pathological oxygen debt is marked by increased anaerobic metabolism (lactate).

3. Key Formulas

A. Arterial Oxygen Content (CaO2)

CaO2 is the total oxygen carried in 100 mL of arterial blood, primarily bound to hemoglobin (Hb).

CaO2 = (Hb √ó 1.34 √ó SaO2) + (PaO2 √ó 0.003)

Where:

  • Hb = hemoglobin concentration (g/dL)
  • 1.34 = H√ºfner's constant (Hb oxygen binding capacity)
  • SaO2 = arterial oxygen saturation (fraction)
  • PaO2 = arterial partial pressure of oxygen (mmHg)
  • 0.003 = solubility constant for oxygen in plasma (mL O2 / dL blood / mmHg)
B. Oxygen Delivery (DO2)

DO2 is the product of CaO2 and Cardiac Output (CO).

DO2 = CO √ó CaO2 √ó 10

  • A factor of 10 converts the result to mL O2 / min from dL (where CO is in L/min).
C. Oxygen Consumption (VO2) (Fick Principle)

VO2 is calculated using the Fick Principle, measuring the difference in oxygen content between arterial and venous blood (CaO2 - CvO2).

VO2 = CO √ó (CaO2 - CvO2) √ó 10

  • CvO2 = mixed venous oxygen content.
  • The difference (CaO2 - CvO2) is the arterio-venous oxygen difference (C(a-v)O2).
D. Mixed Venous Saturation (SvO2)

Mixed venous saturation (SvO2) is an important clinical surrogate reflecting the balance between DO2 and VO2. Low SvO2 (<65%) indicates increased O2ER and often reflects a critical state where DO2 is insufficient to meet VO2 (e.g., shock).
