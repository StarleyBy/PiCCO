PiCCO Algorithms: Key Decision-Making Schemes

PiCCO algorithms provide a structured approach to identifying the specific physiological defect limiting oxygen delivery (DO2) and guide targeted therapy (fluids, inotropes or vasopressors), while minimizing the risk of pulmonary edema (EVLWI).

Hemodynamic Optimization Algorithm

The goal of this algorithm is to maintain oxygen delivery (DO2) above metabolic needs, sequentially optimizing preload, afterload and contractility, with strict control of pulmonary edema risk (EVLWI).

Step 1: Status Check and Goal Setting

Before any intervention, confirm the patient's current status and required goals.

  • Flow and Power: Check Cardiac Index (CI) (Goal ‚â• 2.5) and Cardiac Power Index (CPI) (Goal ‚â• 0.5)
  • Perfusion: Check MAP (Goal ‚â• 65 mmHg) and clinical markers (lactate, diuresis)
  • Safety Check: Check Extravascular Lung Water Index (EVLWI) (Goal ‚â§ 10 ml/kg). If EVLWI is critically high (>15), prioritize fluid restriction/removal

Step 2: Volume/Preload Optimization (Fluid Decision)

Give priority to dynamic indices. Only give fluid if the patient has proven responsiveness and the lungs are in relative safety.

Step 3: Afterload/Resistance Optimization (Vasoactive Decision)

Address pressure-flow issues using SVRI.

Vasodilation/Hypotension SVRI < 1700 AND MAP below goal Vasopressors (e.g., norepinephrine) to increase SVRI to 1700-2500

Step 4: Contractility/Pump Function Optimization (Inotropic Decision)

If CI is still inadequate after optimizing preload and afterload, the problem is the myocardium.

Pump Failure CFI < 4.5 OR GEF < 25% (after GEDVI is optimal) Inotropic Support (e.g., dobutamine) to increase CFI and CI
High Target CI If high VO2 requires CI ‚â• 4.0 (e.g., hyperdynamic sepsis) Use inotropes to achieve high CI target

Step 5: Final Check and Endpoint (Oxygen Transport)

Confirm that all interventions have reached the goal of maintaining hemodynamic stability.

  • Oxygen Delivery Check: Calculate DO2I. If low (<450), re-evaluate all components (CI, Hgb, SaO2)
  • Oxygen Consumption Check: Calculate O2ER. If high (>30%), tissues are struggling; DO2I must be aggressively increased

The optimization process is cyclical. After completing Step 4, return to Step 1 to reassess the patient's status and continue the cycle until achieving hemodynamic stability and maintenance.

Step-by-Step TPTD Calibration Guide

Calibration is an important intermittent process that links continuous hemodynamic monitoring data (pulse contour analysis, PCA) with precise, absolute volumetric and flow values obtained from transpulmonary thermodilution (TPTD).

  1. System Readiness Check (Before Injection)
    • Ensure the patient is in a stable clinical state (or at least stable for the 60-second measurement window)
    • Check that the syringe contains the correct volume of cold NaCl (10-20 mL). Critically important that all air bubbles are carefully removed, as air seriously compromises the accuracy of the thermal signal
    • On the monitor, ensure the displayed injectate temperature (T_inj) is stable and low (ideally ‚â§ 8¬∞C). The system often uses this measurement in its quality checks
    • Check the arterial line: ensure the arterial pressure waveform is stable, properly damped, and free from artifacts
  2. Measurement Activation
    • Select the appropriate function on the monitor (e.g., "Start TPTD", "Calibrate", or "CO")
    • The monitor typically displays a prompt confirming readiness. Prepare for rapid injection based on the system's audio cue or countdown
  3. Indicator Injection (Key Action)
    • Inject the entire volume of cold indicator (10-20 mL) as rapidly and smoothly as possible (ideally within 5-10 seconds)
    • Rationale: Rapid injection ensures the thermal signal is introduced into the central circulation as a compact bolus, maximizing signal-to-noise ratio and ensuring a reproducible thermal curve. A slow injection will smear the curve, leading to underestimation of CO
  4. Thermodilution Curve Analysis
    • The monitor automatically displays the TPTD curve - a plot of temperature versus time recorded by the arterial thermistor
    • The system software instantly analyzes the curve:
      • Area under the curve: used to calculate measured CO_TPTD
      • Mean transit time (MTt): used to calculate GEDV and ITBV
      • Exponential downslope time (DSt): used to calculate EVLW
  5. Quality Control and Averaging
    • Curve Assessment: The monitor has internal quality checks and may reject a curve if the injection was too slow, if there were pressure artifacts (e.g., patient movement) or if T_inj was unstable
    • Repeat Measurements: To ensure statistical reliability and clinical accuracy, at least 3 consecutive, accepted TPTD measurements must be performed
    • Averaging: The system automatically averages the accepted CO values (and correspondingly GEDV and EVLW values). The difference between the accepted CO values should typically be within 10% for the measurement set to be considered valid
  6. Completion and Continuous Monitoring
    • Acceptance: Confirm the averaged, validated values on the monitor (e.g., "Accept Values")
    • PCA Recalibration: The system uses the newly confirmed, accurate measured CO_TPTD values to recalculate and update the calibration factor K for the pulse contour analysis PCA algorithm
    • Return to Continuous Mode: The system automatically returns to displaying continuous, calibrated parameters (PCCO, SVV, PPV) that are now synchronized with the patient's current hemodynamic status

Rules for Recalibration (When to Repeat TPTD)

To prevent "drift" of the PCA coefficient, recalibration is mandatory under the following clinical conditions:

  1. Scheduled intervals: Regularly every 6-8 hours, or according to institutional protocol
  2. Vasopressor/inotrope changes: After any significant initiation, cessation, or major titration (e.g., dose change >20%) of vasoactive drugs (e.g., norepinephrine, vasopressin) or inotropes (e.g., dobutamine). These drugs fundamentally change arterial compliance and vascular tone, making the old calibration factor K invalid
  3. Significant volume shifts: After rapid infusion of large volume fluid (e.g., ‚â•500 mL crystalloid), significant blood loss, or initiation of renal replacement therapy (RRT) involving major volume removal
  4. Clinical data mismatch: If the continuous PCCO or CI displayed on the monitor does not match the patient's clinical state (e.g., patient appears deeply shocked but CI shows normal)

Common Calibration Errors

The accuracy of the PiCCO method relies heavily on a clean, reproducible TPTD curve. Errors introduced during the cold saline injection process or due to technical issues can lead to significant errors in CO, volume (GEDV), and lung water (EVLW) measurements.

Injection and Technique Errors

  • Slow or hesitant injection: Injecting the bolus slowly (over 15-20 seconds) rather than rapidly (‚â§10 seconds) stretches and delays the thermal signal. The area under the curve (CO) is preserved, but the mean transit time (MTt) is artificially prolonged, leading to falsely high GEDVI and ITBVI values
  • Air injection: Failure to completely remove air bubbles from the syringe or injection line. Air acts as a thermal insulator and prematurely dissipates the cold signal in the right heart/pulmonary artery, leading to falsely low CO
  • Warm injectate solution: Using solution that is too warm (e.g., room temperature) or not using external cooling. Insufficient temperature difference (ŒîT) between blood and injectate. The resulting curve is small and noisy, leading to unreliable CO measurement
  • Inconsistent bolus volume: Changing the volume of injectate between the 3 required measurements (e.g., 15 mL, then 10 mL, then 15 mL). Direct calculation error, as the injectate volume (V_inj) is a fixed variable in the Stewart-Hamilton equation, leading to high variability (non-reproducibility) in CO measurements

Algorithm for Differentiating Pulmonary Edema Causes

Using EVLWI and PVPI to differentiate between hydrostatic and permeability pulmonary edema.

Step 1: Assess EVLWI

Check the Extravascular Lung Water Index to determine if pulmonary edema is present.

EVLWI Range (ml/kg PBW) Clinical Interpretation
Normal 3.0 - 7.0
Mild Edema 7.0 - 10.0
Moderate Edema 10.0 - 15.0
Severe Edema >15.0

Step 2: Calculate and Assess PVPI

Calculate PVPI = EVLWI / ITBVI to determine the cause of pulmonary edema.

PVPI Range Clinical Interpretation
Optimal 1.0 - 1.7
High >1.7
