Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) about PiCCO Monitoring

This section answers the most common questions regarding the use of the PiCCO system and interpretation of the obtained data.

II. Volume and Flow

What is the primary purpose of GEDVI?		 To act as a static preload indicator (volume status) that is an excellent surrogate for central venous pressure (CVP).
What does a low CFI (< 4.5) mean? Myocardial dysfunction/low contractility (inotropy). Indicates need for inotropic support (e.g., dobutamine).
What does a high SVRI (> 2500) mean? High afterload/vasoconstriction (e.g., compensatory vasoconstrictor response in cardiogenic shock or high-dose vasopressors).
III. Dynamic Parameters (SVV / PPV)

What is the most common cause of SVV invalidity in an ARDS patient?		 Using low tidal volume (VT < 8 mL/kg IBW), which limits the necessary inspiratory pressure for generating a measurable SVV.
Does spontaneous breathing indicate SVV invalidity? Yes. Even minimal spontaneous efforts disrupt the controlled cyclic pressure changes required for SVV calculation.
What to do if SVV > 13% but GEDVI is normal? The patient still responds to fluid. GEDVI is a static parameter; SVV is dynamic. The patient may need volume despite seemingly optimal static filling.
IV. Lung Water and Permeability

Are EVLWI and PVPI good prognostic indicators?		 Yes. EVLWI closely correlates with severity of lung dysfunction and mortality in ARDS and septic shock.
My patient is septic (PVPI > 1.7), EVLWI is high. What to do? Implement conservative fluid strategy (fluid restriction/removal) to reduce EVLWI while treating sepsis.
V. Oxygenation

What does DO2 measure?		 Oxygen delivery. The total amount of oxygen transported to tissues per minute (CO √ó CaO2). Represents O2 supply.
What does VO2 measure? Oxygen consumption. The total amount of oxygen used by tissues per minute. Represents O2 demand.
What does a high OER (> 30%) mean? Increased oxygen extraction. Tissues maximize extraction to compensate for low DO2 or high VO2. Risk of supply dependency.

Specific Clinical Questions

Below are more complex clinical questions that often arise when using PiCCO in intensive care.

1. How to interpret a profile with high CI and high EVLWI?

This is a classic scenario in septic cardiomyopathy:

  • High CI: Hyperdynamic state, often caused by high heart rate (compensation).
  • High EVLWI: Indicates pulmonary edema caused by a combination of:
    • Permeability edema (PVPI ‚Üë) from sepsis
    • Or excessive previous fluid therapy
  • Low CFI: If CFI is low, this indicates true septic cardiomyopathy (reduced contractility).

Strategy: Aggressive fluid removal to reduce EVLWI, while providing inotropic support if CFI is low.

2. How does PiCCO help in managing a patient with severe burns?

Patients with severe burns are at risk for both hypovolemia and hypervolemia due to capillary leak:

  • Intensive resuscitation: Use GEDVI and SVV to determine adequacy of fluid therapy (volume).
  • Safety control: Use EVLWI as a critical parameter to prevent excessive infusion that will worsen capillary leak and impair lung function.
  • De-resuscitation strategy: After stabilization, use EVLWI to guide removal of excess fluid.

3. How to use PiCCO in a patient with cardiogenic shock?

In cardiogenic shock, the key task is to improve flow without worsening pulmonary congestion:

  • Pump assessment: Check CI and CFI. If CFI is low, the main problem is myocardial dysfunction.
  • Congestion assessment: Check EVLWI and GEDVI. High values indicate excessive loading.
  • Strategy: Use inotropes to improve CFI and CI, while applying diuretics to reduce EVLWI and GEDVI.

4. How to interpret the profile in ARDS?

In ARDS, PiCCO helps differentiate between hydrostatic and permeability edema:

  • High EVLWI + high PVPI: Permeability edema (ARDS). Restrict infusion, aim for minimal GEDVI compatible with adequate perfusion.
  • High EVLWI + normal PVPI: Hydrostatic edema (e.g., from heart failure). Use diuretics to reduce GEDVI and EVLWI.

5. How does PiCCO help in managing a patient after cardiac surgery?

After cardiac surgery, patients often suffer from myocardial stunning and/or hypovolemia:

  • Contractility assessment: Check CFI. Low CFI indicates myocardial stunning requiring inotropic support.
  • Volume assessment: Check GEDVI and SVV. Low GEDVI and high SVV indicate need for infusion.
  • Safety control: Check EVLWI to prevent excessive infusion that may worsen lung function.
