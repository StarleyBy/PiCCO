Dynamic Parameters: Stroke Volume Variation (SVV) and Pulse Pressure Variation (PPV)

Dynamic parameters are key indicators used to predict fluid responsiveness in patients undergoing mechanical ventilation. These include Stroke Volume Variation (SVV) and Pulse Pressure Variation (PPV).

Stroke Volume Variation (SVV)

SVV is the primary dynamic index used to predict fluid responsiveness during controlled mechanical ventilation. It quantifies the change in stroke volume over the respiratory cycle caused by cyclic changes in intrathoracic pressure.

Definition and Calculation

SVV is calculated continuously by the PiCCO system using pulse contour analysis (PCA). It measures the difference between the maximum and minimum indexed stroke volume (SVI) observed during one respiratory cycle, expressed as a percentage of the mean SVI:

SVV = (SVI_max - SVI_min) / SVI_mean √ó 100%

Physiological Mechanism

Mechanical ventilation causes pressure fluctuations that affect venous return and cardiac output:

  1. Inspiration: Increased positive intrathoracic pressure temporarily decreases venous return to the right heart
  2. Effect: This leads to a subsequent decrease in left ventricular preload and, accordingly, to a measurable drop in stroke volume and SVI
  3. Response: If the heart is operating on the steep (preload-dependent) portion of the Frank-Starling curve, even small changes in preload cause large changes in SV, resulting in high SVV

Clinical Significance: Fluid Decision

The key clinical application of SVV is to determine if fluid therapy will lead to a clinically significant increase in cardiac output (CO/CI).

Pulse Pressure Variation (PPV)

PPV is another key dynamic index used to predict fluid responsiveness in mechanically ventilated patients. It measures the maximum change in arterial pulse pressure over one respiratory cycle.

Definition and Physiological Basis

Pulse pressure (PP) is the difference between systolic and diastolic arterial pressure (PP = P_systolic - P_diastolic). PPV quantifies the relative difference between the maximum and minimum pulse pressure observed during mechanical ventilation:

PPV = (PP_max - PP_min) / PP_mean √ó 100%

Clinical Significance: Fluid Response

The interpretation of PPV is identical to SVV and serves to make the decision about fluid administration.

PPV Range (%) Interpretation (Fluid Responsiveness) Therapeutic Decision
High (>10-13%) Responds to Fluid: Indicates hypovolemia Fluid therapy is indicated, expect ‚â•10-15% increase in CI/SV
Low (<10-13%) Does Not Respond to Fluid: Adequate volume status or volume independence Avoid fluid; focus on contractility (CFI) or afterload (SVRI)

Mandatory Conditions for Reliability

SVV and PPV are only accurate predictors when the following strict physiological and technical conditions are met. If any condition is violated, the SVV/PPV value must be ignored.

  1. Fully Controlled Mechanical Ventilation: The patient must be fully sedated or paralyzed without spontaneous breathing efforts. Patient efforts break the cyclic pressure change
  2. Sinus Rhythm: Severe arrhythmias (e.g., atrial fibrillation) create beat-to-beat variability, making SVV/PPV unreliable
  3. Closed Chest/Abdomen: The system relies on fixed transmission of intrathoracic pressure. Conditions such as an open chest compromise this transmission
  4. Sufficient Tidal Volume (V_T ‚â• 8 mL/kg): Tidal volume must be large enough (typically ‚â• 8 mL/kg of predicted body weight) to create sufficient change in intrathoracic pressure. Low tidal volumes (e.g., in ARDS) may lead to falsely low SVV

If these conditions are not met, SVV and PPV should be ignored, and the clinician should revert to using static volumetric parameters (GEDVI, ITBVI) combined with passive leg raising to assess fluid responsiveness.
