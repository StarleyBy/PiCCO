Preload Assessment: Global End-Diastolic Volume Index GEDVI

The Global End-Diastolic Volume Index GEDVI is a key PiCCO parameter for assessing cardiac preload.

Definition and Measurement

Global End-Diastolic Volume GEDV is the sum of the end-diastolic volumes of all four cardiac chambers (right atrium, right ventricle, left atrium, and left ventricle). 

It represents the total compliant blood volume in the heart immediately before systole.

The indexed form, Global End-Diastolic Volume Index GEDVI, is GEDV divided by the patient's body surface area (BSA), expressed in mL/m¬≤. This indexing allows comparison between patients of different sizes.

Measurement via Transpulmonary Thermodilution TPTD

GEDV is a key volume derived from the analysis of the TPTD curve:

  1. Mean Transit Time (MTt): This is the average time for the cold indicator to travel from the injection site (CVC) to the detection site (arterial thermistor)
  2. Calculation: GEDV is derived from cardiac output (CO) and mean transit time (MTt), reflecting the volume contained in the flow path:

GEDV is closely related to Intrathoracic Blood Volume (ITBV), which represents the total blood volume in the heart and entire pulmonary circulation. GEDV typically comprises about 75% of ITBV:

Clinical Significance: Superior Preload Marker

A. Volume vs. Pressure Assessment

GEDVI is considered a superior volumetric marker of preload compared to traditional filling pressures such as Central Venous Pressure (CVP) or Pulmonary Artery Occlusion Pressure (PCWP).

  • Pressure Limitations: CVP and PCWP are heavily dependent on factors unrelated to blood volume, such as:
    • Ventricular Compliance: Stiff ventricular filling (e.g., in sepsis or severe heart failure) can generate high PCWP even with normal or low volume
    • Intrathoracic Pressure: Positive End-Expiratory Pressure (PEEP) or mechanical ventilation significantly elevates CVP and PCWP, causing misinterpretation
  • GEDVI Advantage: GEDVI is a volumetric measurement, making it relatively independent of compliance and lung pressures. It provides a true estimate of the heart's "fill"

B. Fluid Therapy Guidance

GEDVI provides the baseline context for interpreting dynamic parameters (SVV/PPV):

GEDVI Range (mL/m¬≤) Clinical Interpretation Fluid Therapy Strategy
Low (<680) Hypovolemia: True volume deficit (relative or absolute) Fluid therapy indicated: Aggressively pursue volume resuscitation to reach the optimal range
Optimal (680 - 800) Normovolemia: Optimal preload; heart is adequately filled Dynamic assessment required: Check SVV/PPV before additional fluid. Focus shifts to CI (flow) and SVRI (afterload)
High (>800) Hyper-volemia/Fluid Overload: Significant volume reserve, potentially leading to congestion Fluid restriction/removal: Focus on diuretics or fluid removal; avoid additional volume
