Extravascular Lung Water Index EVLWI and Pulmonary Vascular Permeability Index PVPI

The Extravascular Lung Water Index EVLWI is a unique parameter provided by the PiCCO system for objective, quantitative determination of pulmonary edema.

Definition and Measurement of EVLWI

Extravascular Lung Water EVLW is the absolute mass of fluid that has accumulated in the lungs outside the vascular space (i.e., in the interstitium and alveoli). It represents the true measurement of pulmonary edema and is the target for de-resuscitation and fluid restriction in patients with lung injury.

The indexed form, Extravascular Lung Water Index EVLWI, is EVLW divided by the patient's predicted body weight (PBW) or ideal body weight (IBW), expressed in ml/kg. This indexing is necessary to prevent distortion in obese or edematous patients.

Measurement by Transpulmonary Thermodilution TPTD

EVLW is measured intermittently during transpulmonary thermodilution TPTD calibration.

  1. Thermal Indicator: As the cold indicator passes through the lungs, some of the thermal energy exchanges with the extravascular lung water
  2. Downslope Time DSt: The system analyzes the specific characteristics of the thermal curve's downslope, including the Lung Thermal Volume (PTV). Slow heat exchange with water outside the vessels extends the time required for the thermal signal to decay
  3. Calculation: EVLW is calculated based on the difference between the total thermal volume (PTV) and the total blood volume in the lungs (ITBV - GEDV)

EVLW = PTV - (ITBV - GEDV)


Clinical Significance: Superior Assessment of Edema

EVLWI is a powerful tool because it is an objective, quantitative, and global measurement of pulmonary edema.

  • Superiority over Chest X-ray and PCWP:
    • Chest X-ray: Subjective, slow to change, and requires large fluid accumulation (>30%) for visual detection
    • PCWP (Pulmonary Capillary Wedge Pressure): Measures only hydrostatic pressure. EVLW can be high even with normal PCWP (e.g., in ARDS or capillary leak)
  • Guidance for Fluid Restriction in ARDS: In patients with ARDS (Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome), the goal is often to maintain the lowest possible EVLWI compatible with adequate systemic flow
  • Safety Check for Fluid Therapy: EVLWI serves as a critical safety parameter for fluid protocols, warning the physician when aggressive fluid resuscitation primarily contributes to pulmonary congestion rather than systemic flow

Normal Range and Interpretation

EVLWI Range (ml/kg PBW) Clinical Interpretation Clinical Consequence
Normal 3.0 - 7.0 Optimal fluid balance in the lungs
Mild Edema 7.0 - 10.0 Early/subclinical edema; requires careful fluid management
Moderate Edema 10.0 - 15.0 Significant pulmonary edema; strong indication for fluid restriction or removal (diuretics/RRT)
Severe Edema >15.0 Life-threatening pulmonary edema; associated with high mortality and severe PaO2/FiO2 impairment

Pulmonary Vascular Permeability Index PVPI

PVPI is a sophisticated derived parameter used to assist in differentiating the cause of pulmonary edema: whether it is primarily hydrostatic (pressure-driven) or capillary leak (permeability).

Definition and Calculation

PVPI is the ratio of indexed extravascular lung water EVLWI to indexed intrathoracic blood volume ITBVI:

PVPI = EVLWI / ITBVI

  • Rationale: This ratio compares the volume of fluid outside the vessels (EVLWI) with the volume of blood inside the central vessels (ITBVI). 
  • If PVPI is high, it suggests that fluid is leaving the vessels disproportionately to the volume of blood inside, indicating a permeability problem. 
  • If PVPI is normal, the edema is likely due to hydrostatic forces (high pressure)

Normal Range and Cautious Interpretation

PVPI Range Clinical Interpretation
Optimal 1.0 - 1.7
High >1.7
